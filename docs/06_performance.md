# 6. Performance

**Question:** Is our rating distribution healthy or inflated? Where is top talent concentrated?

---

## Key Findings

**JustKaizen's performance rating distribution is healthy and well-calibrated, with 54% of employees rated "Meets Expectations" and a clean bell-curve shape.** However, the 31% top performer rate (employees rated 4+ or flagged as critical talent) creates a tension: nearly one-third of the workforce is classified as high-value talent, yet the company lost 27 of these employees in Q1 2026 alone. Top talent is concentrated in Engineering (142 top performers) and Sales (68), both of which are experiencing elevated attrition. The question is not whether the distribution is healthy -- it is whether the company is retaining the talent it has identified as most valuable.

---

## Rating Distribution (2025 Year-End Review)

1,319 employees received ratings in the 2025 Year-End Review Cycle:

| Rating | Count | % |
|--------|-------|---|
| Significantly Exceeds Expectations | 85 | 6.4% |
| Exceeds Expectations | 250 | 19.0% |
| Meets Expectations | 716 | 54.3% |
| Partially Meets Expectations | 211 | 16.0% |
| Does Not Meet Expectations | 57 | 4.3% |

This is a well-shaped distribution. The 54% concentration at "Meets Expectations" indicates that managers are not inflating ratings -- a common problem in tech companies where 70%+ of employees often receive the top two ratings. The 20% of employees rated below "Meets" (Partially Meets + Does Not Meet) suggests the calibration process is functioning: managers are differentiating performance rather than defaulting everyone to the middle or top.

**Benchmark context:** Industry research from Mercer and WorldatWork suggests that a healthy distribution has 50-60% in the middle tier, 20-30% in the top two tiers, and 10-20% in the bottom two. JustKaizen's distribution (25.4% top, 54.3% middle, 20.3% bottom) falls within these ranges.

## Top Performer Concentration

403 employees (30.6%) are classified as top performers (rating of 4+ or critical talent flag).

| Executive | 9 | 15 | 60% |
| People | 34 | 84 | 40% |
| G&A | 38 | 116 | 33% |
| Engineering | 142 | 449 | 32% |
| Data & Analytics | 18 | 59 | 31% |
| Sales | 68 | 229 | 30% |
| Product | 29 | 104 | 28% |
| Marketing | 28 | 113 | 25% |
| Customer Success | 37 | 150 | 25% |

Engineering has the largest absolute pool of top performers (142), followed by Sales (68). These are also the two largest departments by headcount. The concentration matters because the attrition data from [Section 2](02_attrition.md) shows that Engineering lost 9 top performers and Sales lost 4 in Q1 2026 alone. At that rate, Engineering would lose 36 top performers annually -- 25% of its top talent pool -- purely through voluntary attrition.

Customer Success and Marketing have the lowest top performer rates at 25%. This could reflect genuine performance distribution differences, or it could indicate that the calibration standards in these departments are more conservative. Either way, these departments also have the lowest average ratings (CS: 2.94, D&A: 2.93), which may warrant a calibration review to ensure consistency across departments.

## The Retention Problem for Top Talent

The performance data becomes urgent when combined with attrition:

| Metric | Q1 2026 |
|--------|---------|
| Total departures | 104 |
| Top performer departures | 27 (26%) |
| Top performer departures that were regrettable | 20 (19% of all departures) |
| Senior Leadership departures that were top performers | 4 of 5 (80%) |
| Director-level departures that were top performers | 3 of 9 (33%) |

The company has invested in identifying and rating its top talent through a structured review process. The problem is not identification -- it is retention. 27 top performers left in a single quarter. If the annual top performer attrition rate exceeds 20%, the company is effectively losing its best people faster than it can develop replacements.

**The cost of top performer attrition is disproportionate.** Research from the Center for American Progress estimates that replacing a highly skilled employee costs 100-150% of their annual salary when factoring in recruiting costs, onboarding time, and productivity ramp. For 27 top performers at JustKaizen's average salary of $145,191, the estimated replacement cost is $3.9M to $5.9M for Q1 2026 alone.

---

## Recommended Actions

1. **Implement a top performer retention program.** The 27 top performers who left in Q1 2026 should be treated as a pattern, not individual events. Proactive retention actions for the remaining 376 top performers could include: retention bonuses tied to 12-month commitments, accelerated promotion timelines, expanded scope or stretch assignments, and direct compensation adjustments for those below band midpoint.

2. **Cross-reference top performer status with compensation band position.** Of the 30 below-band engineers identified in [Section 4](04_compensation.md), determine how many are top performers. Below-band top performers are the single highest flight risk cohort in the company and should be prioritized for immediate compensation correction.

3. **Review calibration consistency between departments.** The 25% top performer rate in Customer Success vs. 40% in People warrants examination. If CS is applying a stricter standard, top performers in CS may not be receiving the recognition and investment they deserve, which compounds the career opportunity attrition driver.

4. **Track top performer attrition as a board-level metric.** Overall attrition rate treats all departures equally. Top performer attrition rate isolates the loss that matters most. Present this metric quarterly alongside regrettable attrition rate to give leadership a clear view of talent risk.
