# 2. Attrition

**Question:** Are we losing people faster than we should be? Are we losing the right or wrong people?

---

## Key Findings

**JustKaizen is losing people at an accelerating rate, and over half of those losses are regrettable.** The 3-month annualized attrition rate reached 33.4% in Q1 2026, more than triple the rate from one year ago. The 12-month trailing rate stands at 22.2%, confirming this is a sustained trend and not a seasonal blip. Of 104 departures in Q1 2026, 54 (52%) were regrettable and 27 (26%) were top performers. The problem is most acute in Customer Success, Sales, and Engineering -- the same three departments driving the headcount decline.

---

## Attrition Trend

Attrition has been climbing steadily since mid-2025, accelerating sharply into Q1 2026:

| Quarter | TTM Overall | TTM Voluntary | R3M Annualized | R3M Vol. Annualized |
|---------|------------|---------------|----------------|---------------------|
| 2024 Q1 | 4.3% | 4.1% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| 2024 Q2 | 8.2% | 7.8% | 19.3% | 18.6% |
| 2024 Q3 | 9.4% | 8.7% | 10.2% | 8.3% |
| 2024 Q4 | 9.7% | 9.0% | 10.1% | 9.8% |
| 2025 Q1 | 12.4% | 11.5% | 9.9% | 9.6% |
| 2025 Q2 | 11.0% | 10.3% | 13.6% | 13.3% |
| 2025 Q3 | 13.3% | 12.9% | 18.9% | 18.5% |
| 2025 Q4 | 16.4% | 15.8% | 22.8% | 21.1% |
| **2026 Q1** | **22.2%** | **21.1%** | **33.4%** | **31.1%** |

The TTM rate provides the structural view: attrition has roughly doubled every 12 months since 2024 Q2. The R3M annualized rate shows the recent acceleration: the current quarter is running at a pace that, if sustained for a full year, would mean losing one-third of the workforce.

For context, the tech sector median for voluntary attrition is typically 13-15% annually. JustKaizen's TTM voluntary rate of 21.1% is already well above industry norms, and the quarterly pace suggests it is still accelerating.

## Q1 2026 Departures: The Composition

Of 104 total terminations in Q1 2026:

| Category | Count | % of Total |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| Voluntary | 97 | 93% |
| Involuntary | 7 | 7% |
| Regrettable | 54 | 52% |
| Top Performer | 27 | 26% |
| Top Performer + Regrettable | 20 | 19% |

This is overwhelmingly a voluntary attrition problem. The involuntary terminations (7) are routine performance management. The voluntary departures (97) are employees choosing to leave, and more than half of those departures are people the company wanted to keep.

### Top Performers Are Leaving

26% of all Q1 departures were top performers (rated 4+ or flagged as critical talent). By level group:

| Level Group | Terminations | Top Performers | TP % |
|------------|-------------|---------------|------|
| Junior IC (P1-P3) | 47 (45%) | 12 | 26% |
| Senior IC (P4-P6) | 25 (24%) | 5 | 20% |
| Manager (M1-M2) | 18 (17%) | 3 | 17% |
| Director (M3-M4) | 9 (9%) | 3 | 33% |
| Senior Leadership (E1+) | 5 (5%) | 4 | 80% |

The Senior Leadership number is small but alarming: 4 of 5 executive-level departures were top performers. At the Director level, one-third of departures were top performers. These are the hardest roles to backfill and the most disruptive to lose.

## Why People Are Leaving

The top voluntary termination reasons in Q1 2026:

| Reason | Count | % of Total |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Career Opportunities | 37 | 36% |
| Work-Life Balance | 19 | 18% |
| Compensation | 16 | 15% |
| Manager Relationship | 10 | 10% |
| Personal Reasons | 7 | 7% |
| Other / Misc | 15 | 14% |

Career Opportunities as the top reason at 36% signals a structural problem, not individual dissatisfaction. When employees consistently cite career growth as the reason for leaving, it typically means one of three things: the internal promotion pipeline is too slow, the role has limited scope for growth, or competitors are offering titles and responsibilities that JustKaizen is not.

## Department Deep Dive

### Customer Success: 42% R3M Annualized Attrition

Customer Success lost 15 employees in Q1 2026, with 9 (60%) classified as regrettable and 5 (33%) as top performers. The sub-department breakdown:

| Sub-Department | R3M Annualized | Top Reason |
|---------------|----------------|------------|
| CSM | 63.6% | Career Opportunities (53%) |
| Customer Education | 52.9% | Career Opportunities |
| Support | 44.0% | Career Opportunities |

**The pattern:** Over half of CS attrition is driven by career opportunities. CSMs in particular are leaving at a rate that would turn over the entire team in under two years. This role typically sits at P2-P3 with limited promotion paths within CS. The 53% career opportunity reason strongly suggests these employees are leaving for Senior CSM, Team Lead, or Customer Success Manager roles at companies with clearer career ladders.

**Revenue impact:** CSMs carry account portfolios. Each departure creates a coverage gap, increases load on remaining CSMs, and introduces churn risk for the accounts in transition.

### Sales: 37% R3M Annualized Attrition

Sales lost 19 employees, with 7 (37%) regrettable and 4 (21%) top performers.

| Sub-Department | R3M Annualized | Top Reason |
|---------------|----------------|------------|
| Account Management | 53.6% | Manager Relationship |
| Sales Engineering | 52.2% | Career Opportunities |
| SDR | 43.8% | Career Opportunities |
| Enterprise Sales | 29.3% | Career Opportunities |
| Account Executive | 29.3% | Work-Life Balance |

**The pattern:** Sales attrition is driven by a different mix than the rest of the org. Manager Relationship is the top reason at 26% for the department overall (vs. 10% org-wide). This is a leadership quality signal. The Account Management team has the highest attrition rate at 54% and the highest concentration of manager-related departures, which points to a specific management problem rather than a systemic one.

**Revenue impact:** Account Managers own renewal and expansion revenue. SDRs feed the top of the pipeline. Sales Engineering departures slow deal velocity. All three losses directly compress revenue capacity.

### Engineering: 36% R3M Annualized Attrition

Engineering lost 38 employees (the most of any department), with 20 (53%) regrettable and 9 (24%) top performers.

| Sub-Department | R3M Annualized | Top Reason |
|---------------|----------------|------------|
| DevOps & SRE | 46.2% | Career Opportunities |
| Infrastructure | 44.7% | Career Opportunities |
| AI/ML | 41.7% | Career Opportunities |
| Platform | 39.0% | Career Opportunities |
| Data Engineering | 33.1% | Career Opportunities |
| Developer Experience | 28.6% | Career Opportunities |

**The pattern:** Career Opportunities dominates at 32%, followed by Work-Life Balance at 24%. Every Engineering sub-department has the same top reason. This is not a team-specific problem -- it is an Engineering-wide competitiveness problem. These roles (DevOps, Infrastructure, AI/ML) are among the most in-demand in the tech market. When combined with Engineering's below-average compa-ratio (0.992 vs. 1.000 org-wide) and a 50% offer acceptance rate for Engineering roles, the picture is clear: JustKaizen's total compensation package for engineers is not competitive with what the market is offering.

## Recommended Actions

1. **Conduct stay interviews in Customer Success and Engineering.** These two departments have the highest regrettable attrition rates among large teams. The data says "career opportunities" is the reason -- stay interviews will reveal what specific opportunities are pulling people away and what JustKaizen would need to offer to compete.

2. **Audit the management layer in Sales, specifically Account Management.** Manager Relationship as the top reason at 26% is an outlier relative to the rest of the org (10%). This suggests a localized leadership problem that can be addressed through coaching, reassignment, or structural changes.

3. **Review Engineering compensation against market data.** Engineering's compa-ratio of 0.992 combined with Career Opportunities as the universal departure reason and a 50% offer acceptance rate all point to the same conclusion: the compensation package is not competitive. See [Section 4: Compensation](04_compensation.md) for detail.

4. **Build career progression frameworks for CSM and SDR roles.** These are high-attrition, early-career roles where the top reason for leaving is career opportunities. A visible promotion path (CSM to Senior CSM to CS Manager, SDR to AE) with defined timelines and criteria would directly address the retention driver.

5. **Present the regrettable attrition rate to leadership as the primary workforce metric.** Overall attrition rate includes departures the company is comfortable with. The 52% regrettable rate isolates the problem to its most actionable form: more than half the people leaving are people the company wants to keep.

*Data source: fct_attrition_reporting, fct_employee_roster. TTM = trailing twelve-month rate. Regrettable classification is assigned at time of termination by the departing employee's manager. Top performer = most recent performance rating of 4+ or critical talent flag.*
