# 7. Cross-Domain Insights

**Question:** Where do workforce signals compound across domains?

---

## Why This Section Exists

Individual domain analyses answer individual questions: attrition rates, offer acceptance rates, compa-ratios, engagement scores. But the most actionable insights emerge when signals from multiple domains converge on the same population. A department with high attrition is a concern. A department with high attrition, low offer acceptance, below-midpoint compensation, and declining engagement is a crisis with a clear intervention point.

This section identifies the three populations where cross-domain signals compound most urgently.

---

## Signal 1: Engineering -- A Compounding Talent Crisis

Engineering is the largest department (410 employees, 34% of the workforce) and the one where every workforce signal points in the same direction.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 36% R3M annualized rate, 38 departures in Q1, 20 regrettable (53%), 9 top performers | Critical |
| Hiring | 50% offer acceptance rate, 59.2-day avg time-to-fill, 44 offers to close 22 hires | Critical |
| Compensation | 0.992 compa-ratio (below org avg of 1.000), 30 employees below band | Contributing |
| Engagement | Recognition score 3.55 (declining), Career Growth 3.56 (org-wide low) | Reinforcing |
| Performance | 142 top performers (32% of dept), Career Opportunities = top reason across all sub-depts | At risk |

**The diagnosis:** Engineering employees are underpaid relative to band, underrecognized per survey data, and leaving for career opportunities at every level and in every sub-department. The company is then unable to close half the candidates it identifies as replacements, likely because the same compensation and scope gaps that drive attrition also make offers uncompetitive. This is a self-reinforcing cycle: attrition increases load on remaining engineers, which worsens work-life balance (the #2 departure reason at 24%), which accelerates further attrition.

**The intervention:** This is fundamentally a compensation and career development problem. The data supports three concurrent actions: (1) bring all 30 below-band engineers to midpoint immediately, (2) conduct a market analysis to determine if Engineering bands themselves need adjustment, and (3) publish a visible career progression framework for engineering roles with clear criteria for P2-to-P3, P3-to-P4, and P4-to-Senior Staff promotions. These address the top two departure reasons (Career Opportunities and Compensation) directly.

**Estimated cost of inaction:** At the current quarterly rate of 9 top performer departures, Engineering would lose 36 top performers annually. At an estimated replacement cost of $145K-$218K per top performer (100-150% of avg salary), the annual cost of Engineering top performer attrition alone is $5.2M-$7.8M. A targeted compensation adjustment for 30 below-band engineers would cost a fraction of this.

## Signal 2: Customer Success -- Engaged People Leaving for Better Opportunities

Customer Success presents a counterintuitive pattern: the second-highest engagement score in the organization paired with the highest annualized attrition rate.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 42% R3M annualized rate, 15 departures, 9 regrettable (60%), 5 top performers (33%) | Critical |
| Hiring | 45.5% offer acceptance rate (lowest in org), only 6 hires in Q1 vs. 15 departures | Critical |
| Compensation | 0.996 compa-ratio, 12 employees below band (9%), Compensation = #2 departure reason (20%) | Contributing |
| Engagement | 3.74 avg score (2nd highest), Recognition declining (-0.09) | Paradox |
| Performance | 25% top performer rate (lowest), 2.94 avg rating (lowest) | Calibration question |

**The diagnosis:** Customer Success employees like their work but are leaving because the external market offers more money and better career paths. The CSM role (64% annualized attrition) is the epicenter -- a role that typically sits at P2-P3 with limited promotion visibility. Career Opportunities accounts for 53% of CS attrition, and Compensation is #2 at 20%. These employees are not disengaged; they are simply getting better offers.

The 45.5% offer acceptance rate -- the lowest in the organization -- means Customer Success also cannot replace the people it is losing. The department shrank from 139 to 130 in a single quarter and is on a trajectory to fall below 120 by mid-year without intervention.

**The intervention:** (1) Adjust compensation for the 12 below-band CS employees, prioritizing CSMs. (2) Build a career ladder for the CSM role: CSM to Senior CSM to CS Manager to Director of CS, with defined timelines and promotion criteria. (3) Investigate why the performance calibration in CS is producing the lowest top performer rate (25%) and avg rating (2.94) -- if CS managers are applying a stricter standard, high performers in CS may be underrecognized relative to peers in other departments, compounding the "career opportunities" pull.

## Signal 3: Sales Account Management -- A Leadership Problem, Not a Pay Problem

Account Management within Sales has the department's highest attrition rate (54% R3M annualized) but a *different* root cause than Engineering or Customer Success.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 54% R3M annualized, Manager Relationship = top reason in Sales at 26% | Critical |
| Compensation | Sales dept compa-ratio 1.008 (above org avg) | Not a factor |
| Engagement | Sales overall: 3.49 (lowest in org) | Reinforcing |
| Headcount | Account Management: 41 to 36 (-12.2%) in one quarter | Severe |

**The diagnosis:** Unlike Engineering and Customer Success, Sales attrition is not driven by compensation. Sales has the second-highest compa-ratio in the org at 1.008. The problem is manager quality. Manager Relationship is the #1 departure reason in Sales at 26%, more than double the org-wide rate of 10%. Account Management is the most affected sub-department, losing 12% of its headcount in a single quarter. The lowest overall engagement score in the org (3.49) confirms that Sales employees are broadly less satisfied with their work environment.

**The intervention:** This requires a people-manager intervention, not a structural one. (1) Conduct 360-degree feedback for all Account Management managers. (2) Review manager spans of control -- if Account Management managers are overloaded, the relationship quality suffers mechanically. (3) If specific managers are driving the attrition concentration, address through coaching, development plans, or reassignment. The good news is that manager-driven attrition is typically the most responsive to intervention: fix the manager, fix the retention.

---

## Summary: Three Populations, Three Different Interventions

| Population | Root Cause | Primary Intervention | Secondary |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Engineering (410 employees) | Compensation + career path | Market comp adjustment + career ladders | Recognition programs |
| Customer Success (130 employees) | Career path + compensation | CSM career ladder + below-band correction | Calibration review |
| Sales Account Management (36 employees) | Manager quality | 360 feedback + coaching/reassignment | Engagement action plan |

The common thread is that each population's problem has a *specific, identifiable cause* in the data. JustKaizen does not have a generic "retention problem." It has three distinct retention problems, each requiring a different solution. Treating them with the same intervention (e.g., a broad compensation increase or a company-wide engagement initiative) would overspend on the wrong lever for two of the three populations.
