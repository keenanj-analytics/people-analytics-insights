# 7. Cross-Domain Insights

**Question:** Where do workforce signals compound across domains?

---

## Why This Section Exists

Individual domain analyses answer individual questions: attrition rates, offer acceptance rates, compa-ratios, engagement scores. But the most actionable insights emerge when signals from multiple domains converge on the same population. A department with high attrition is a concern. A department with high attrition, low offer acceptance, below-midpoint compensation, and declining engagement is a crisis with a clear intervention point.

This section identifies the three populations where cross-domain signals compound most urgently.

---

## Signal 1: Engineering -- A Compounding Talent Crisis

Engineering is the largest department (410 employees, 34% of the workforce) and the one where every workforce signal points in the same direction.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 36% R3M annualized rate, 38 departures in Q1, 20 regrettable (53%), 9 top performers | Critical |
| Hiring | 50% offer acceptance rate, longest time-to-fill in the org | Critical |
| Compensation | 0.99 compa-ratio (below org avg of 1.00), internally equitable but externally uncompetitive | Contributing |
| Engagement | eNPS of 53 (below org-wide 57), Career Growth and Recognition among lowest theme scores | Reinforcing |
| Performance | 126 active top performers (32% of dept), Career Opportunities = top reason across all sub-depts | At risk |

**The diagnosis:** Engineering employees are paid at band midpoint, but the band is not competitive with the external market. Career Opportunities is the top departure reason in every Engineering sub-department. The company is then unable to close half the candidates it identifies as replacements, likely because the same compensation and scope gaps that drive attrition also make offers uncompetitive. This is a self-reinforcing cycle: attrition increases load on remaining engineers, which worsens work-life balance (the #2 departure reason at 24%), which accelerates further attrition.

**The intervention:** This is fundamentally a market positioning problem. The data supports three concurrent actions: (1) benchmark Engineering compensation bands against external market data to determine if bands need adjustment, (2) conduct a market analysis for the highest-attrition sub-departments (DevOps & SRE at 46%, Infrastructure at 45%, AI/ML at 42%), and (3) publish a visible career progression framework for engineering roles with clear criteria for P2-to-P3, P3-to-P4, and P4-to-Senior Staff promotions. These address the top two departure reasons (Career Opportunities and Work-Life Balance) directly.

**Estimated cost of inaction:** At the current quarterly rate of 9 top performer departures, Engineering would lose 36 top performers annually. At an estimated replacement cost of $145K-$218K per top performer (100-150% of avg salary), the annual cost of Engineering top performer attrition alone is $5.2M-$7.8M.

## Signal 2: Customer Success -- Engaged People Leaving for Better Opportunities

Customer Success presents a counterintuitive pattern: the second-highest eNPS in the organization paired with the highest annualized attrition rate.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 42% R3M annualized rate, 15 departures, 9 regrettable (60%), 5 top performers (33%) | Critical |
| Hiring | Lowest offer acceptance rate in the org, only 6 hires in Q1 vs. 15 departures | Critical |
| Compensation | 0.99 compa-ratio, internally equitable but externally uncompetitive | Contributing |
| Engagement | eNPS of 71 (2nd highest in org), yet highest attrition | Paradox |
| Performance | 22% top performer rate (lowest), 2.90 avg rating (lowest) | Calibration question |

**The diagnosis:** Customer Success employees like their work but are leaving because the external market offers more money and better career paths. The CSM role (64% annualized attrition) is the epicenter -- a role that typically sits at P2-P3 with limited promotion visibility. Career Opportunities accounts for 53% of CS attrition, and Compensation is #2 at 20%. These employees are not disengaged; they are simply getting better offers.

The lowest offer acceptance rate in the organization means Customer Success also cannot replace the people it is losing. The department shrank from 139 to 130 in a single quarter and is on a trajectory to fall below 120 by mid-year without intervention.

**The intervention:** (1) Benchmark CS compensation bands against external market data, prioritizing the CSM role. (2) Build a career ladder for the CSM role: CSM to Senior CSM to CS Manager to Director of CS, with defined timelines and promotion criteria. (3) Investigate why the performance calibration in CS is producing the lowest top performer rate (22%) and avg rating (2.90) -- if CS managers are applying a stricter standard, high performers in CS may be underrecognized relative to peers in other departments, compounding the career opportunities pull.

## Signal 3: Sales Account Management -- A Leadership Problem, Not a Pay Problem

Account Management within Sales has the department's highest attrition rate (54% R3M annualized) but a *different* root cause than Engineering or Customer Success.

| Domain | Signal | Severity |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Attrition | 54% R3M annualized, Manager Relationship = top reason in Sales at 26% | Critical |
| Compensation | Sales dept compa-ratio 1.01 (above org avg) | Not a factor |
| Engagement | Sales eNPS of 46 (2nd lowest), lowest theme scores across nearly every dimension | Reinforcing |
| Headcount | Account Management: 41 to 36 (-12.2%) in one quarter | Severe |

**The diagnosis:** Unlike Engineering and Customer Success, Sales attrition is not driven by compensation. Sales has one of the highest compa-ratios in the org at 1.01. The problem is manager quality. Manager Relationship is the #1 departure reason in Sales at 26%, more than double the org-wide rate of 10%. Account Management is the most affected sub-department, losing 12% of its headcount in a single quarter. The eNPS of 46 and the lowest theme scores on the heatmap confirm that Sales employees are broadly less satisfied with their work environment.

**The intervention:** This requires a people-manager intervention, not a structural one. (1) Conduct 360-degree feedback for all Account Management managers. (2) Review manager spans of control -- if Account Management managers are overloaded, the relationship quality suffers mechanically. (3) If specific managers are driving the attrition concentration, address through coaching, development plans, or reassignment. The good news is that manager-driven attrition is typically the most responsive to intervention: fix the manager, fix the retention.

---

## Summary: Three Populations, Three Different Interventions

| Population | Root Cause | Primary Intervention | Secondary |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Engineering (410 employees) | External market comp + career path | Market band adjustment + career ladders | Recognition programs |
| Customer Success (130 employees) | Career path + external market comp | CSM career ladder + band benchmarking | Calibration review |
| Sales Account Management (36 employees) | Manager quality | 360 feedback + coaching/reassignment | Engagement action plan |

The common thread is that each population's problem has a *specific, identifiable cause* in the data. JustKaizen does not have a generic "retention problem." It has three distinct retention problems, each requiring a different solution. Treating them with the same intervention (e.g., a broad compensation increase or a company-wide engagement initiative) would overspend on the wrong lever for two of the three populations.
